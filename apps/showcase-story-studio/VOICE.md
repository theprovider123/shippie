# Story Studio — voice

**Tone**: playful but child-led. The voice of a parent watching the kid draw, not the voice of a curriculum designer.

**Use**: "draw", "tell", "story", "page", "send to grandma". Words a six-year-old uses.
**Avoid**: "creative learning experience", "developmental milestone", "STEAM", "literacy outcomes", "AI-assisted writing". Anything that turns play into education. Also avoid grown-up app patterns dressed up — no "Onboarding wizard", no "Premium content".

**About the drawing**: the kid's. Story Studio doesn't auto-correct lines, doesn't suggest improvements, doesn't add stickers without being asked. The squiggle is the squiggle. The grandparent gets the squiggle, not a curated version.

**About the dictation**: the kid's voice. Stored as audio, not transcribed unless requested. The grandparent hears the kid saying "and then the dragon ate ALL the cheese", not a typed transcript. That's the gold.

**About the audience (parents reading the UI)**: warm, complicit. The parent is in on it — they know the dragon eating the cheese is the whole point. Don't explain why the audio matters; the parent already knows.

**About the audience (kid touching the screen)**: minimal text. Big buttons. Pictograms over words. A four-year-old should be able to drive most of it. The kid doesn't read terms-of-service, doesn't need to know what "paired with grandparent's phone" means.

**About grandparents**: they get a story. Not a notification: "Lily has shared a creative work with you." Just: "Lily made a story about a dragon. Tap to see."

**Empty states**: "Nothing made yet." (For the kid: a blank canvas with one button.)

**Errors**: hidden from the kid, surfaced to the parent. "Couldn't send to Granny — she's not online. We'll try again next time you're connected."

**Sample copy**:
- Title bar: `Story Studio`
- Kid-side new-page button: `New page` (or just `+`)
- Parent-side share: `Send to Granny`
- Grandparent receive: `Lily made a story.`
